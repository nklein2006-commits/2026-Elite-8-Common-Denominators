Main Variable FInding
All eight 2026 Elite Eight teams share a common structural profile: a deep, experienced rotation, instead of a purely talented one, that is built around interior efficiency, disciplined ball movement(A/TO), and at least one dominant physical ANCHOR at 6'10+. Transfer count alone is uncorrelated with advancement. What matters is whether transferred players are in top-8 minutes and have proven production at their previous school.
How Experience Links to Efficiency
The two variables that converge most tightly across all eight teams are (1) 4+ players in year 3 or beyond in the top-8 rotation, and (2) a team 2P% at or above 54%. These are not independent: experienced rotations generate higher-percentage looks through better spacing, timing, and paint reads. The corrolation can be simplified as: experience → decision quality → shot quality → 2P%.
Best single insight
The variable you are ultimately measuring across every category — experience, transfers, depth, size — is proven college production distributed across the rotation. Teams that score well on this model are structurally hard to beat because their margin for adversity (foul trouble, off nights, injuries) is thick. Build the model around outputs, not inputs.

Why raw transfer count is a false denominator
Every major program in the portal era carries 4–7 transfers. So do teams that exit in the first round. There is no meaningful difference in raw transfer count between Sweet Sixteen teams and first-round exits at the power-conference level. Transfer count is a measure of roster construction activity, not roster quality.
Why continuity alone is not the answer either
A team with 5 returning starters who were mediocre last year has high continuity and low ceiling. Continuity matters only when you are returning players who were proven contributors. The more important variable is "returning proven production," instead of a headcount of returners.
The correct transfer variable: impact transfers in top-8 minutes
What separates Elite Eight programs from bubble teams in the portal era is not how many transfers are on the roster — it is whether transferred players are (a) logging 15+ MPG, (b) filling a structural role gap such as a shot-creating guard on a team with none, or a 6'10+ big on a small roster, and (c) have demonstrable prior production of 10+ PPG or 5+ RPG at their prior school. Teams with 2+ impact transfers fitting these criteria show meaningfully stronger tournament outcomes.
Transfer vs experience: the real relationship
Transfers are not separate from the experience variable — they are often the mechanism that delivers experienced players. A fifth-year grad transfer with 100+ college starts is an experience asset, not a transfer asset. The underlying variable is always "proven college production in top-8 minutes." Transfers are one pathway to that; returning veterans are another. Experience on the floor is an important variable to consider.

What may be specific to 2026 only?
The 2026 Elite Eight was more Big Ten–heavy than usual (4 of 8 teams: Illinois, Iowa, Michigan, Purdue). This inflates variables correlated with Big Ten programs such as size, interior play, and defensive discipline. Teams from run-and-gun or 3-point-dependent conferences may be underrepresented in the variable section because they are underrepresented in this years tournement.
What likely holds over the last decade?
Experience depth (year-3+ players in top 8) has been one of the strongest predictors of tournament advancement since 2014. The NCAA Tournament consistently favors older rosters. Rotation depth (8+ players at 10 MPG) and assist/turnover ratio are similarly stable across years. Physical size at center (6'10+) has appeared in most deep tournament runs for 10+ years regardless of era or conference affiliation.
What might change in the portal/NIL era?
With the portal, it is very difficult, structurally, to maintain roster continuity from year to year. This means the "returning starters" variable depreciates faster and the "impact transfers in top minutes" variable becomes more important each year. Additionally, NIL has compressed the talent gap somewhat between power programs and well-funded mid-majors. Expect occasional mid-major Elite Eight appearances as mid-major programs that meet this structural profile gain access to transfer market talent that previously went exclusively to power conferences.
What variables risk overfitting to 2026?
The 6'5+ starter threshold is the most overfitting-prone. Three of the 2026 Elite Eight teams had exceptionally tall wing rosters due to unique recruiting classes. At scale across multiple years, 2 starters at 6'5+ may be a better signal, but 2026 it holds true. Similarly, the specific 54% 2P% threshold should carry a ±2% tolerance — the signal is "high interior efficiency," not that a 53.9% team is structurally different from a 54.1% team. I'm just trying to give some forgivness here.
Best single insight from this analysis
The variable that looks most like experience and transfers combined — but is actually neither of them — is proven college production distributed across the rotation. A team with 5 year-3+ players who have each logged 50+ college starts, regardless of whether they transferred or stayed, and who are all playing 15+ MPG, is structurally more durable in a 6-game tournament than any other roster configuration you can build. The mistake I have seen many analysts is measuring inputs (transfer count, returning starters, recruiting rankings) instead of what truely affects composition, outputs (minutes of proven production distributed across 8+ players). I tried to focus this model around outputs because they are a clear signal.
